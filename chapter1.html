The clock on the dashboard shows 2:00 AM.

The numbers glow dimly, as if unsure whether they should exist here. Incense smoke still floats inside the car, thin and stubborn. You lower the window and let the cold cut through the warmth. The smoke thins, loosens, slips out into the night. It takes its time, but it does leave.

Something else stays.

Her words return the way smells do, without permission, without warning.

You had laughed when she said it. Superstition spoken softly so it would not sound like fear. You had promised her you would be careful anyway, promised like people do when they do not intend to listen.

The air outside is sharper than it should be. October air is supposed to carry dampness, not bite. This cold feels older, heavier, like it belongs to a different calendar. You look past the windshield. The road is barely visible. Fog presses against the headlights, swallowing light instead of reflecting it.

An hour ago, you were still connected to the world. Signal bars. Music playing low. A road that made sense.

Then the fuel needle fell. Not slowly. Not honestly. One moment it hovered, the next it collapsed. The engine coughed, resisted, surrendered. No warning light. No final mercy.

Now there is no internet. No signal. No proof that anything exists beyond the fog.

You reach under the seat and pull out the map.

The paper is thick, yellowed, folded too many times along the same lines. You recognize the creases the way one recognizes scars. She had told you about it once, casually, as if it were a joke. You had found it days later on your doorstep, untouched by rain, untouched by dust. Only the map, and words written in a careful, deliberate hand.

The map ends an hour behind you. Beyond that, nothing.

Google Maps never worked.

The fog thickens before you realize the road has ended. Not broken. Not cracked. Simply gone.

The car idles longer than it should. Headlights pour into white nothingness. When you step out, cold wraps around you immediately. The air smells wet, faintly rotten, with a trace of salt that does not belong inland.

Water moves nearby. You hear it breathing against mud.

The map told you to go straight.

Now there is no straight.

Only a narrow strip of earth sinking forward. Your shoes press into ground that yields slightly, as if it remembers being water. On one side, something tall and tangled blocks the light. On the other, emptiness.

The fog eats distance. It eats direction.

You turn. The car already feels far away, like a thought you abandoned.

Wooden posts rise from the ground ahead. Old. Leaning. Their tops blackened by rain and time. No writing you can read. No lights from any village. Even sound feels careful here.

You think of the words again.

Maa. Dakai.

They were never meant to explain. Only to summon.

Movement stirs ahead. Not footsteps. Something sliding where land should resist it. The fog opens just enough for water to reveal itself, a narrow channel widening like a mouth.
